Hey,

His first session back, he did 25 minutes. Half what he'd planned.

Afterwards he sent me a message. I expected the usual. "Felt good to be back" or "legs are killing me." What he actually wrote was this:

"I've been treating every session like it needs to be perfect or it doesn't count."

That sentence changed how I coach people.

He was right. He'd been measuring every session against some ideal version in his head. A full hour. The right exercises. Enough intensity to feel destroyed afterwards. And when the gap between that ideal and what he could actually face on a Tuesday morning got too wide, he chose nothing.

Your brain is binary about this. If the task feels too big, it picks the easier option. Every time. That's 300,000 years of wiring. Your nervous system doesn't care about your gym membership. It cares about conserving energy and avoiding situations where you feel out of your depth.

The fix is stupid simple. Lower the bar until showing up is the win.

Here's something you can do tonight: lay out a t-shirt and shorts where you'll see them in the morning. Not packed in a bag for a full session. Just on the chair. The decision to train starts twelve hours before you get there. Make the first step so small your brain doesn't bother fighting it.

That's how it works in theory. But I want to tell you what it actually looks like when someone lives it for six months. A client of mine made one small change to his commute and it quietly rewired his whole week.
