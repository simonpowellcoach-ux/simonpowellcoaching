The commute story.

A client of mine, finance guy, early forties, started coaching because he wanted to lose weight. He'd done it before. Gym memberships, apps, a mate's training plan off the internet. He knew what to do. Always had.

Three weeks in, things were fine. Training. Ticking off sessions on the app. Eating better. But his step count was stuck around 3,000 a day. Desk job, train to work, train home, sit down. The lunchtime walk he kept meaning to take never happened.

Then one Thursday his wife was out. He'd normally ride the train all the way home, but he had 3,000 steps to do and nothing waiting. So he got off two stops early, walked to the shop, picked up a couple of steaks, walked the rest home.

That was it. That was the moment.

A twenty-minute walk burns about 80 calories. Meaningless. What mattered was the decision. He chose the harder option when the easier one was right there. And the next morning he woke up and felt good about it.

He started doing it twice a week. Then meal prepping on Sundays. Then he noticed he was sleeping better. About four months in, someone offered him a drink at a work thing and he said no. He told me it hadn't felt like willpower. It just wasn't what he wanted anymore.

Six months later: down a stone and a half, training five days a week, eating properly six out of seven. The one day off is what he calls "sanity insurance." His words.

He didn't get there because I wrote him a better programme. Programmes are easy. He got there because he stopped seeing himself as someone who tries to get fit and became someone who trains. That shift sounds small. It is the entire game.
